## Understanding AI Well Enough to Explain It

How would you define AI?

AI is a nebulous term. It simultaneously refers to technologies, innovations, implementations, and tools. Fundamentally, it is the idea, science, and engineering of simulating human intelligence. Practically speaking, it is a set of methods and systems that enable computers to mimic facets of intelligence, including learning, synthesis, reasoning, and classification.

Alan Turing posed the famous question "Can machines think?" in his paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," where he proposed whether a machine is distinguishable from a human as a proxy for measuring a machine's intelligence, or its ability to think. This was called the "Imitation Game" and is now more widely known as the Turing Test.

This posited human behavior as the ideal for intelligence. The equivalence proved to be incredibly productive and operationalizable — it's a lot easier to measure whether a computer program is distinguishable from a human (a yes/no question) than measuring how intelligent a system is.

The term "artificial intelligence" was first invoked in a proposal for a workshop by John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon. The "Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence" took place in the summer of 1956, and is considered the moment AI was established as a field.

A major facet of human intelligence is the ability to learn from data. **Machine Learning** is a branch of computer science and AI that focuses on enabling computers to learn from data, as opposed to requiring explicit programming for each task.

For a more comprehensive timeline, see [A Brief History of Artificial Intelligence](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/history-of-artificial-intelligence) by IBM.

## Key Terms

**Artificial Intelligence (AI)**
The idea, science, and engineering of simulating human intelligence.

**Machine Learning (ML)**
A branch of computer science and artificial intelligence that enables computers to learn from data, as opposed to requiring explicit programming for each task.

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)**
A subset of machine learning focused on enabling computers to interpret and generate human language.

**Generative AI**
A subset of machine learning that can generate new content such as text, images, and music.

**AI / ML Model**
A general term for computer programs that learn from data to make predictions, classify information, or generate content.

**Language Models (LMs)**
AI models that take an input in natural language (a prompt) and produce a relevant output (a response) in natural language. Large Language Models (LLMs) have billions of parameters; Small Language Models (SLMs) have millions or hundreds of millions and tend to be more computationally efficient. These models power AI chatbots.

**AI Applications / Tools / Assistants**
Products with one or more AI models in their architecture and a user-friendly interface — such as ChatGPT.

**Model Context Protocol (MCP)**
An open-source standard that allows AI applications to connect directly to external tools and data sources. Before MCP, each connection between an AI tool and an external system had to be custom-built. MCP replaces that with a universal standard: once a data source has an MCP server, any MCP-compatible AI application can connect to it directly. See the [Academic MCP Guide and Directory](https://github.com/AI-Librarians/academic-mcp-guide) for background and a curated list of servers relevant to academic work.

## How It Works

At its core, a language model generates outputs by predicting the next word (or token) in a sequence. It doesn't "know" facts — it generates what it thinks is most likely based on its training data and the context provided. This is why hallucinations often contain partial truths: the model is producing plausible-sounding text, not retrieving verified information.
